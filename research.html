Drape

Experimenting with Non-Layered Robotic Draping

Virginia Tech, Center for Design Research, Fall 2020

3D printing has grown in popularity and scale since the inception of stereolithography in 1984. Now additive manufacturing is employed in the construction of full-scale buildings, yet the process maintains the same exact methods at small and large scale. Standard 3D printing processes consist of building up an object layer by layer in flat two-dimensional planes. However, current advanced fabrication methods allow for even more complex printing like six-axis printing, form draping, and other non-standard printing processes.

Drape is the exploration of non-layer based additive manufacturing through robotic form draping. The material used for testing is fiber-reinforced PETG pellets. These pellets are dried and fed through the large thermoplastic extruder to create a molten thread of plastic. This material remains molten and malleable for two to three minutes. Draping this material produces a rope-like catenary curve that becomes a rigid body in a matter of minutes. Similar to the chain models of Gaudi, but unique in that the print maintains its rigidity when flipped upside down.

The preliminary testing involved experimenting with material deposition over varying unsupported distances. The wider the unsupported distance the further the material droops. Interestingly, if the supporting material was plywood the material drooped further down than if the supporting material was the previous layer of PETG. Large unsupported distances created more cylindrical objects, causing the droops to hit the ground and completely flatten.

The next round of testing will involve draping over previously hardened material and non-rectilinear forms. The end goal is to create a lightweight human-scale object or structure.
